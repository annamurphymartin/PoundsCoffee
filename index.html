Community, Culture and Coffee
A look at Pounds Coffee

Anna-Murphy Martin

 “It was a beautiful day…we were just sipping on the coffee, watching golf or something.” For Andrew Gross, 29, of Monroe, Georgia, the mention of his first tasting of Pounds Coffee is accompanied by a found memory of an afternoon in the fall of 2015. Gross was one of the first people to taste Pound just a few months after Chris Barr, 31, originally of Jacksonville, Florida, sold his first bag of micro-roasted coffee on April 18, 2015. Pounds is a coffee roasting company out of Watkinsville, Georgia, that specializes in single-origin coffee roasting, packaging, and shipping across the United States. Subscribers receive freshly roasted coffee in their mailbox to brew at home as frequently as best fits their lifestyle and coffee drinking habits. Although only technically operating for two years, Pounds Barr said, “Since 2008 or 2009 I’ve kind of always been dreaming of Pounds. I didn’t know what Pounds was. I didn’t have a name for it. I didn’t entirely know the business plan or model or anything like that, but I knew that I wanted a coffee company. I knew that I wanted it to be mine in some way.” The company has grown to fulfill that personal touch that Barr dreamed of through the message of intentionality that Pounds sells alongside roasted coffee beans from Ethiopia or Guatemala. Subscribers who have formed relationships with Barr through Pounds understand the message. Gross, who has been a Pounds subscriber since that fall day in 2015 said, “with Pounds Coffee you have to be intentional. And that’s something we don’t really see a lot of, intentionality.” The message behind the bean differentiates Pounds from other coffees, but the message is not the only difference from typical coffee in the break room of every office building. Pounds’ coffee beans are “single origin,” which means that they all derive from the same field and harvest. Large, massively produced coffees come from nine or ten different harvests as to keep consistent flavors from year to year, harvest to harvest. This way, if two of the beans must be sourced from a different crop due to drought, pests, or changes in contracts with farmers, the coffee that a family has been brewing daily for fifty years is not suddenly unrecognizable. For companies who function for consistency, this method is effective, but as gourmet coffees rise in popularity, single origin coffees rise in consumption. According to the National Coffee Association, 62 percent of Americans today drink coffee daily, a rise from 57 percent last year. The most shocking static from the survey relates to the daily consumption of gourmet coffee amongst 25-39-year-olds. Of this age demographic, 50 percent claim to drink at least one gourmet coffee beverage each day.

 The National Coffee Association considers gourmet coffee to be defined as “blank (I HAVE CONTACTED THE NCA AND THEIR DIRECTOR OF RESEARCH IS SUPPOSED TO GET BACK TO ME BY THE END OF THE WEEK. I WILL MAKE ADJUSTMENTS TO THE STATISTICS MENTIONED ABOVE AS NEEDED BASED ON THEIR DEFINITION OF “GOURMET”).” A trend towards the increase in coffee consumption is good news for Pounds, but unfortunately also leads towards increases in competition. Similarly to the peak in frozen yogurt shops circa 2014, coffee shops popularity has rising steadily with an annual growth rate of 4.1 percent from 2012 to 2017 according to IBISWorld. IBISWorld also found that the retail market for coffee rose 3.6 annually from 2011 to 2016. Frozen yogurt sales “are expected to keep expanding through at least 2019” according to the market research conducted by Franchise Help. If the trends between the two specialty markets continue to mimic one another, demand of retail coffee can also be expected to continue to grow for a few more years. Economical trends should not just be considered by business owners hoping to grow or distinguish themselves from others in the future when companies consolidate or close. Trends in consumer spending affect the way everyday Joes’ cup of joe will be marketed, advertised, and developed. For Athens-area coffee companies like Jittery Joes, investing and engaging with the local market is the most logical economical option. Pounds’ market, however, while influenced by Athenian consumers, reaches beyond geographical barriers. Pounds has the advantage of engaging with subscribers nationwide via mail and social media. Social media serves as the barrier between business and personal relationships surrounding it. The micro-roasting method that Pounds and other smaller, subscriber-based firms take is naturally more personal than massively roasted coffee. The actual process of micro-roasting is not significantly different than roasting hundreds of pounds of coffee at one time. The difference is the repeated idea of intentionality. Every batch that Barr roasts of Pounds Coffee can be specifically linked to a customer. “Every roast I put in I know the name of the person who I’m going to be bagging it for…someone that I have had the opportunity to grow in relationship” These relationships, based on an online subscription to coffee delivered by mail, are just one aspect of the community that coffee creates throughout the United States and internationally. How do roasted beans that are ground with water filtered through create community? .

Psychologists Lawrence E. Williams and John A. Bargh of University of Colorado at Boulder and Yale University, prospectively, conducted an experiment to find the relationship of physical warmness and feelings of interpersonal warmth. Interpersonal “warmness,” as defined by the study related to the “perceived favorability of the other person’s intentions towards us, including friendliness, helpfulness, and trustworthiness.” Participants were primed with either hot or iced coffee when they were asked to hold the cup on an elevator ride with a research assistant before completing a survey about ten personality traits of a randomly described person. The only differentiation was the physical temperature of the coffee, but it lead to significant differences in responses about the interpersonal judgments towards the target person’s emotional warmness. Science, therefore, proves that drinking a hot coffee in the company of others creates not just physical warmth, but also emotional warmth towards one another. So why then did Krystal D’Costa’s article in Scientific American present evidence that “the number who are [at coffee houses] solely for social purposes seems very small?” Just because the physical warmth of coffee does promote emotional closeness and relationship building doesn’t mean that t is utilized that way. That is why Pounds’ mission of encouraging intentionality each morning is a work in progress. Coffee has evolved from consumption of bland flavors only for caffeine’s sake to a part of daily adult life. Now it is moving towards a social influence with options for everyone, but still is utilized by many for the boost of energy that it can provide. Consumed in-home, Pounds, and other subscription-based coffees, can serve as either part of the morning routine or something to gather around with friends and family. Either way, intentionality as it relates to coffee provides grounds for community to be built. Even if a morning cup of coffee is consumed alone, “it feels more personal” in someone’s home rather than in a coffee shop, according to Amanda Weaver, 19, of Oxford, Georgia.
